While each of the samples were being strain, the stress in the sample was measured and an X-scattering intensity image was taken roughly every four seconds. The twelve samples varied systematically between difference in densities, processing conditions, and thickness. ![Data Image](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/davidbrough1/ME_8883/master/14943264377_6685b8f7e5_z.jpg) **Click to see video** ### Dataset The table below shows the density, thickness and processing condition for the twelve samples. |Density (gm/cc)|Thickness (Âµm)|Processing Condition |:---:|:---:|:---:| |0.912| 20 | A | |0.912| 30 | A | |0.912| 75 | A | |0.912| 20 | B | |0.912| 30 | B | |0.912| 75 | B | |0.923| 20 | A | |0.923| 30 | A | |0.923| 75 | A | |0.923| 20 | B | |0.923| 30 | B | |0.923| 75 | B | In this project we worked towards generating structure-processing linkage between the polyethylene samples and the strain applied to each sample. # Challenges A considerable amount of time was spent trying to recover the 2-point statistics from the Small Angle X-ray Scattering (SAXS) images. Although we understood the need to take a Fourier transform of the image (a shown in [this](http://phelpsforpresident.github.io/MIC-XRD-Polymer/2014/09/17/2-Point-Statistics-and-Xray-Scattering.html) post as well as [this](http://phelpsforpresident.github.io/MIC-XRD-Polymer/2014/10/15/Autocorrelation-from-X-ray-Scattering-Data.html) post), we were unclear how to interpret the physical meaning of these images in real space. Late in the semester, we were able to meet with [Dr. Garmestani](http://www.mse.gatech.edu/faculty/garmestani) from the Materials Science and Engineering department at Georgia Tech. In [previous](http://dx.doi.org/doi:10.1016/j.actamat.2011.01.013) research his group was able to recover pair correlation function for a 2-phase material from SAXS data. From this information, we were able to [derive](http://nbviewer.ipython.org/github/phelpsforpresident/MIC-XRD-Polymer/blob/gh-pages/notebooks/Relationship_between_XRD_and_Autocorrelation.ipynb) the relationship to get the 2-point statistics from the SAXS images. $$ f^{11}( \pmb x) = \frac{1}{V \eta_o^2} \int I ( \pmb k) e^{-i\pmb k\pmb x} d\pmb k + V_1^2 $$ Unfortunately because we found this relationship too late in the semester to use the 2-point statistics for the linkage, but we are interested in using the 2-point statistics to do [microstructure reconstruction](dx.doi.org/doi:10.1016/j.actamat.2007.10.044) from the SAXS images in the future. # Collaboration [Abhiram](https://github.com/phelpsforpresident) comes from the Materials Science and Engineering department, while [David](https://github.com/davidbrough1) comes from the Computational Science and Engineering department. We believe we have a good enough understanding to generate the 2-point statistics as this project continues. Originally PCA was used on the entire X-ray scattering image to do dimensionality reduction. This turned out to be somewhat slow because of the size of the images and because of some of the data structures were initially being used in our code. Later the [dimeion_reducer](https://github.com/phelpsforpresident/MIC-XRD-Polymer/blob/gh-pages/python_scripts/dimension_reducer.py) class was created that would allow for flexibility in selecting a dimensionality reduction technique. This class was designed to integrate well with machine learn python package [scikit-learn](http://scikit-learn.org/stable/). ![Originial Image](https://farm6.staticflickr.com/5591/15083317630_cbdcb13c46_z_d.jpg) **Original Scattering Image** After trying several variations on PCA, we found that [kernel PCA](http://scikit-learn.org/stable/auto_examples/decomposition/plot_kernel_pca.html) with a linear kernel gave us the same reduced representation an order of magnitude faster than [PCA](http://scikit-learn.org/stable/auto_examples/decomposition/plot_pca_iris.html). We also explored [sparse PCA](http://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/generated/sklearn.decomposition.SparsePCA.html) and found that is gave similar results to PCA and kernel PCA, but we not as fast as kernel PCA. Because the signal strength for each image is very low, the log of every pixel was used. In order to account for the variation in X-ray scattering intensity due to sample in thickness, we divided each image by the mean intensity. Before doing PCA, we removed the areas of the images that did not contain scattering from the polyethylene. ![X-ray Scattering image](https://farm9.staticflickr.com/8628/15799068949_9ff2e93ec5_z_d.jpg) **Image used in Kernel PCA** We attempted to use a completely new framework for determining a structure-processing relationship. [Previously](dx.doi.org/doi:10.1016/j.ijplas.2010.03.010) a numerical integration approach has been used to construct these linkages. In this project we used a statistical model know as [transfer function model](http://www.le.ac.uk/ec/research/RePEc/lec/leecon/dp11-15.pdf). The transfer function model can be thought of as a method to use regression for digital filter design for time varying signals. In our case, we found that a model with the order (2, 1, 5) gave us the best result. The 2 represents the number of autoregressive terms in the model, 1 represents the number of times the time series were differenced and 5 represents the previous time steps used from an exogenous (input) series, in our case we used the measured strain values. Our model equation took the following form. $$ X_t= a_1 X_{t-1} + a_2 X_{t_2} + b_0 \varepsilon_t + b_1 \varepsilon_{t-1} + b_2 \varepsilon_{t-2} + b_3 \varepsilon_{t-3} + b_4 \varepsilon_{t-4} + b_5 \varepsilon_{t-5} + Err$$ In equation above, $X_t$ is value for a given principle component over time, and $\varepsilon_t$ is the measured strain for the sample. Because the trajectories for the low density samples has different characteristics than the trajectories for the high density samples, we took on sample from each of the and fit the coefficients of our model to them. Below are a few images of the predicted processing paths compared to original data. ![Predict 1](https://farm8.staticflickr.com/7530/15819938050_552c6195d9_z_d.jpg) ![Predict 2](https://farm8.staticflickr.com/7489/15981419186_fb17031049_z_d.jpg) **Prediction for Low Density Samples** ![Predict 3](https://farm9.staticflickr.com/8655/16005230541_d72bc57de2_z_d.jpg) ![Predict 4](https://farm8.staticflickr.com/7517/15821193499_831bbff413_z_d.jpg) **Prediction for High Density Samples** The model performed reasonable well for the low density samples, but do not preform well for the high density samples when the trajectories had discontinuities. Overall the transfer function model did show some promise as a method to find structure processing linkages. More work will have to be done to explore improvements. # Conclusion This project provided a great foundation for continued collaboration with this dataset. A new method to find structure processing linkages was used. In the future we hope to be able to do microstructure reconstruction as well as find a perhaps a better statistical model to predict these trajectories in the low dimensional representation. # Final Presentation
